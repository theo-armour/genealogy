## James Whitehill Shirk (July 16, 1832 – February 10, 1873) was a distinguished United States Navy officer who served with significant gallantry during the American Civil War

Though your records list his birth year as 1831, naval history records typically cite his birth date as July 16, 1832. He died in Washington, D.C., at the age of 40 (often cited as 41 in biographical contexts depending on the month), with his death widely attributed to health complications resulting from his arduous wartime service.

Here is a summary of his life and career:

### **Military Service (Civil War)**
Shirk is best known for his command of Union gunboats on the "Western Waters" (the Mississippi, Tennessee, and Cumberland rivers). His leadership was critical in several turning points of the war.

* **Battle of Shiloh (April 1862):** This was his most famous action. Commanding the timberclad gunboat **USS *Lexington***, Shirk (along with Lt. William Gwin of the USS *Tyler*) provided crucial fire support that saved Union forces from being overrun.
  * As Confederate troops pushed General Grant’s army back toward the Tennessee River, Shirk moved the *Lexington* into position and shelled the Confederate lines relentlessly.
  * General Grant specifically praised Shirk in his reports, crediting the gunboats with checking the enemy's advance and helping to turn the tide of the battle.
* **Other Major Campaigns:**
  * **Fort Henry:** He participated in the capture of this strategic fort.
  * **Vicksburg Campaign:** He later commanded the ironclad **USS *Tuscumbia*** during the attacks on Vicksburg. During the battle of Grand Gulf, he engaged Confederate batteries for five hours, an action his superiors called one of the "best stand-up fights" of the war.

### **Personal Life & Family**
* **Wife:** Mary W. Shirk (née Mary W. Marshall; they married around 1861/1862).
* **Children:** He left a widow and three children. One notable son was **James Carson Marshall Shirk** (born 1865 in Philadelphia), who later became a prominent architect in Philadelphia.
* **Post-War:** After the war, Shirk rose to the rank of Commander. He served in the European Squadron and on special duty for the Navy Department in Washington, D.C., where he worked until his premature death in 1873.

### **Summary of Significance**
James W. Shirk belongs to a small group of "brown-water navy" officers whose close-range support of Army ground forces was instrumental in securing Union control of the Mississippi River Valley. His legacy is particularly tied to the salvation of the Union Army at Pittsburg Landing (Shiloh).
