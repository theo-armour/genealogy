===Armour Name History===

The surname Armour (also spelled Armorer, Armer, or Armor) has a rich history rooted in Scotland, reflecting occupational, cultural, and linguistic influences. Below is a detailed exploration of its origin, meaning, and historical context, based on available sources and genealogical insights.
Origin and Meaning

Occupational Roots: The surname Armour derives from the Old French word armure, meaning "armor," introduced to Scotland during the Norman influence in the 11th–12th centuries. It originally referred to someone who made, repaired, or wore armor, such as a blacksmith, armorer, or soldier. The Middle English term armurer and Scottish armour directly tie to this trade, akin to surnames like Smith or Fletcher.

Scottish Context: In Scotland, Armour is classified as an occupational surname, common among families associated with metalwork or military roles. The Dictionary of American Family Names (Oxford University Press, 2003) notes it as "Scottish and northern Irish: from Middle English, Old French armure ‘armor’, hence an occupational name for a maker of armor or a nickname for someone who wore armor."

===Historical Development in Scotland===

Early Records: The Armour surname appears in Scottish records as early as the 16th century. The query references Dauid (David) Armour (b. 1546, Canongate, Edinburgh), suggesting the name was established in Midlothian by this time. Variants like Armorer are recorded in early charters, such as the 1296 Ragman Rolls, where individuals swore fealty to Edward I of England, though no Armours are explicitly named there.

Geographic Spread: The surname is strongly associated with western Scotland, particularly Ayrshire (e.g., Kilmarnock, Fenwick) and Argyll (e.g., Campbeltown), as seen in the lineage from Alexander Armour (b. 1655) to Archibald Armour (b. 1782). The House of Names indicates that Ayrshire was a key hub, with families like the Armours tied to local industries such as textiles, farming, and later whisky still production (copper smithing in Campbeltown).

Clan Connections: While not directly tied to a major clan, the Armours in Argyll lived in Campbell territory, suggesting possible affiliations with Clan Campbell. The Forebears database notes the surname’s prevalence in Argyll and Bute, with 1881 UK Census data showing 2,057 Armours, predominantly in Scotland (1,171 in Ayrshire alone).

===Name Evolution===

Spelling Variations: The surname appears as Armour, Armorer, Armer, and Armar in historical records. The Surname Database explains that Scottish parish records often varied due to phonetic spelling by clerks (e.g., Hew Armour, b. ~1590). The shift from Armorer to Armour reflects a simplification over time, especially post-1700s.

Cultural Significance: In medieval Scotland, armorers were valued for crafting protective gear, especially during clan conflicts and wars with England (e.g., Wars of Independence). The surname likely denoted skilled artisans or minor gentry, as seen with Robert Armour (b. 1692), a farmer at Trodigal Farm linked to whisky still production (Kintyre Magazine).

Migration Influence: By the 19th century, Armours like George Armour (b. 1812, d. 1881) migrated to the United States, carrying the name to Chicago, where it became associated with business (grain elevators) and diplomacy (Norman Armour Sr). This reflects the Scottish impact on the surname’s global spread.

===Name Distribution===

Scottish Concentration: Forebears ranks Armour as the 5,202nd most common surname globally, with high incidence in Scotland (1 in 2,058 people in 1881). Ayrshire and Argyll were primary regions, with 74.9% of UK Armours in Scotland per British Surnames.

Global Spread: The 1881 Census shows 2,057 Armours in the UK, with 1,171 in Scotland. By 2014, Forebears estimates 7,376 bearers worldwide, with significant populations in the US (3,409) and Canada, reflecting 19th-century emigration, as seen with George Armour’s move to Chicago.

===Notable Historical Figures===

Early Armours: David Armour (1546–1625) in Edinburgh and Alexander Armour (1655–1740) in Kilmarnock represent early bearers tied to urban and rural trades.

American Branch: George Armour (1812–1881) and his brother Philip Danforth Armour (founder of Armour & Company, meatpacking) elevated the name’s prominence in the US.

===Limitations and Notes===

Record Gaps: Pre-1700 records (David and Hew Armour) rely on user-submitted trees (Ancestry, Geni), with limited primary sources like parish registers. Verification is recommended.

Variant Confusion: The Armorer/Armour shift can complicate tracing, as both appear in Ayrshire records.

Cultural Context: The name’s occupational origin aligns with Scotland’s martial and craft traditions

Conclusion

The Armour surname in Scotland originates from the Old French armure, denoting an armorer or armor-wearer, emerging as an occupational name in the medieval period. Centered in Ayrshire and Argyll, it evolved from artisan roots to encompass farming and trade, with families like those in Campbeltown tied to whisky production. The name’s spread to the US in the 19th century reflects Scottish emigration patterns. Early records are sparse, but the lineage from David (1546) to modern bearers like Norman Theo Armour III (b. 1947) shows continuity and adaptation.
